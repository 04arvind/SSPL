Elementary Statistics

1. What is Statistics?

Statistics is a branch of mathematics that deals with the collection, classification, presentation, analysis, and interpretation of numerical data.

  • Collection of Data: Gathering information or observations (e.g., heights of students in a class).
  • Organization of Data: Arranging raw data into a systematic form like a frequency table.
  • Presentation of Data: Displaying data using graphs, histograms, bar charts, or pie charts.
  • Analysis: Finding patterns, trends, or averages (like mean, median, mode).
  • Interpretation: Drawing conclusions or inferences based on the analyzed data.

Application: Statistics is widely used in real life — business, economics, medicine, sports, social science, and more. In exams, students often solve problems related to measures of central tendency (mean, median, mode).

2. Measures of Central Tendency

Measures of Central Tendency are values that represent the center or typical value of a dataset. The three most common measures are:

  • Mean (Arithmetic Mean):
    It is the average of all observations.
    Formula: Mean = (Sum of all observations) ÷ (Total number of observations)
    Example: For data 4, 6, 8, 10:
    Sum = 4 + 6 + 8 + 10 = 28; Number of terms = 4
    Mean = 28 ÷ 4 = 7
  • Median:
    The median is the middle value when the data is arranged in ascending or descending order.
    • Odd number of terms: Median = Middle term
    • Even number of terms: Median = (Sum of two middle terms) ÷ 2
    Example (Odd): For 3, 5, 7, Median = 5
    Example (Even): For 3, 5, 7, 9, Median = (5 + 7)/2 = 6
  • Mode:
    The mode is the number that occurs most frequently in a dataset.
    Example: For data 2, 4, 4, 6, 7 → Mode = 4 (since 4 appears twice)
    Note: A dataset can be:
    • Unimodal: One mode
    • Bimodal: Two modes
    • Multimodal: More than two modes
    • No mode: If all values occur with the same frequency

Applications: These measures help summarize large data sets with a single representative value and are commonly used in data analysis, economics, and exam questions.

3. Example 1: Mean

Find the mean of 2, 4, 6, 8, 10.

  • Sum = 2 + 4 + 6 + 8 + 10 = 30.
  • Mean = 30 / 5 = 6.
  • Answer: 6.

4. Example 2: Median

Find the median of 3, 1, 7, 4, 9.

  • Ordered: 1, 3, 4, 7, 9.
  • Median = 4.
  • Answer: 4.

5. Example 3: Mode

Find the mode of 2, 3, 3, 4, 5, 3.

  • 3 appears 3 times.
  • Answer: Mode = 3.

6. Example 4: Combined

Find mean, median, mode of 5, 7, 7, 8, 10.

  • Mean = (5 + 7 + 7 + 8 + 10) / 5 = 7.4.
  • Median: Ordered = 5, 7, 7, 8, 10; Median = 7.
  • Mode = 7.
  • Answer: Mean = 7.4, Median = 7, Mode = 7.

7. Example 5: Find Mean, Median, and Mode

Question: Find the mean, median, and mode of the following dataset:
12, 15, 12, 18, 19, 12, 22, 25, 19

  • Step 1 – Arrange in order: 12, 12, 12, 15, 18, 19, 19, 22, 25
  • Mean: (12 + 15 + 12 + 18 + 19 + 12 + 22 + 25 + 19) = 154 ÷ 9 = 17.11
  • Median: Middle value = 18 (5th value in ordered list)
  • Mode: Most frequent = 12 (appears 3 times)

Answer: Mean = 17.11, Median = 18, Mode = 12

8. Example 6: Mean from Frequency Table (Grouped Data)

Question: The marks of 40 students are given below. Find the mean:

Marks (Class Interval) Frequency (f)
0 – 104
10 – 206
20 – 3010
30 – 4012
40 – 508

Step 1 – Midpoints (x): 5, 15, 25, 35, 45

Step 2 – Calculate f × x:

  • 4×5 = 20, 6×15 = 90, 10×25 = 250, 12×35 = 420, 8×45 = 360

Total f = 40, Σ(fx) = 1140

Mean = Σ(fx)/Σf = 1140/40 = 28.5

9. Practice Problems

  1. Find the mean of 1, 3, 5, 7, 9.
  2. Find the median of 4, 2, 8, 6, 10.
  3. Find the mode of 1, 2, 2, 3, 3, 3, 4.
  4. Find mean, median, mode of 12, 15, 15, 17, 20.
  5. If mean of 5 numbers is 10 and sum is 50, find one number if others are 8, 9, 10, 12.

10. Solutions

  1. Mean = 25 / 5 = 5.
  2. Median = 6.
  3. Mode = 3.
  4. Mean = 15.8; Median = 15; Mode = 15.
  5. Fifth number = 11.

11. Tips

  • Sort data for median.
  • Check frequency for mode.
  • Verify mean by summing all values.
