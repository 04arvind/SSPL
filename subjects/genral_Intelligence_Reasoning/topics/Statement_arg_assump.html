Statement – Arguments and Assumptions

1. Introduction

In logical reasoning, questions based on arguments and assumptions test your ability to assess reasoning behind a statement. These types are frequently asked in competitive exams to evaluate analytical thinking and decision-making abilities.

2. What is a Statement?

A statement is a sentence that expresses a fact, opinion, suggestion, or idea. It forms the basis of the question in both arguments and assumptions.

3. Understanding Arguments

An argument is a reason or a set of reasons given to support or oppose a statement. It can be:

  • Strong Argument: Directly related, logical, and important to the statement.
  • Weak Argument: Not directly related, based on emotional grounds, or irrelevant consequences.

Tip: Focus on whether the argument supports the core of the statement logically.

4. Understanding Assumptions

An assumption is something that is not stated but is taken for granted or assumed to be true in the context of the statement.

Tip: Ask: "What must be true for the statement to make sense?"

  • Look for hidden beliefs or underlying thoughts.
  • Assumptions should be implicit and essential.

5. Strategy to Solve Argument Questions

  1. Read the statement and argument carefully.
  2. Identify whether the argument is supporting or opposing.
  3. Evaluate whether the argument is relevant and logically connected.
  4. Eliminate emotional or extreme arguments.

6. Strategy to Solve Assumption Questions

  1. Read the statement carefully.
  2. Ask yourself what must be assumed for this to be valid.
  3. Assumptions should be reasonable and not far-fetched.
  4. Use elimination to discard invalid assumptions.

7. Practice Questions (Arguments)

Statement: Should the government ban all vehicles older than 15 years?

Argument I: Yes, they cause more pollution.

Argument II: No, people cannot afford new vehicles.

Answer: Argument I is strong; Argument II is weak (emotional).

Statement: Should online classes be made permanent for all universities?

Argument I: Yes, it is more flexible for students and teachers.

Argument II: No, not all students have access to good internet.

Answer: Both arguments are strong.

8. Practice Questions (Assumptions)

Statement: "Use hand sanitizers frequently to stay safe from viruses." — An advisory.

Assumption I: Hand sanitizers kill viruses.

Assumption II: People want to stay safe from viruses.

Answer: Both assumptions are implicit and valid.

Statement: "Government should make yoga compulsory in schools."

Assumption I: Yoga has health benefits.

Assumption II: Students will oppose yoga classes.

Answer: Only Assumption I is valid.
