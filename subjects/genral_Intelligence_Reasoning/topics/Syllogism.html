Syllogism - General Intelligence and Reasoning

1. Introduction to Syllogism

Syllogism is a logical reasoning topic that involves drawing conclusions from two or more categorical statements (premises). Each statement describes a relationship between categories (e.g., "All A are B"). The task is to determine which conclusions logically follow. Syllogisms test deductive reasoning and are common in competitive exams to assess logical thinking.

Objective: Evaluate given premises to identify valid conclusions or determine if a specific conclusion is true.

Applications: Used in philosophy, mathematics, and decision-making processes.

Historical Context

The concept of syllogism was introduced by the ancient Greek philosopher Aristotle in the 4th century BCE. It formed the foundation of what we now call classical logic. In his treatise “Prior Analytics”, Aristotle defined syllogism as a kind of logical argument that applies deductive reasoning to arrive at a conclusion based on two or more premises.

A typical example of a classical syllogism is:
All humans are mortal.
Socrates is a human.
Therefore, Socrates is mortal.

In this structure, the first two statements (called premises) lead to a third statement (called the conclusion), which logically follows. This form of reasoning allowed early thinkers to formalize arguments and distinguish between valid and invalid conclusions — a revolutionary step in the history of logic and reasoning.

Over time, syllogisms became central to Western philosophy, especially during the Middle Ages, when scholars used Aristotelian logic in theology and academic debates. In India, similar forms of structured reasoning were developed independently in schools of logic like Nyaya, which also emphasized inference (anumana) and premise-based conclusions.

Today, syllogistic reasoning is a core part of reasoning sections in competitive exams like RRB, SSC, UPSC, and banking tests. It helps students understand deductive logic, improve critical thinking, and evaluate the validity of arguments — essential skills in both academics and real-world decision-making.

2. Types of Syllogism Questions

Syllogism questions can be classified based on the nature of premises and conclusions:

  • Standard Syllogism: Two premises with categorical statements (e.g., All, Some, No).
  • Possibility Questions: Determine if a conclusion is possible (e.g., "Some A may be B").
  • Negative Statements: Include "No" or "Some not" statements.
  • Coded Syllogism: Statements use symbols or codes for categories.
  • Three or More Premises: Complex syllogisms with multiple statements.
  • Complementary Pair Questions: Check pairs like "Some A are B" and "Some A are not B".

3. Key Concepts in Syllogism

Understanding categorical statements is essential:

Statement Type Description Venn Diagram
All A are B Every A is included in B A circle inside B circle
No A are B No A is included in B Separate A and B circles
Some A are B At least one A is B Overlapping A and B circles
Some A are not B At least one A is not B A circle with part outside B

Terms:

  • Major Premise: First statement (e.g., All A are B).
  • Minor Premise: Second statement (e.g., All B are C).
  • Conclusion: Derived statement (e.g., All A are C).
  • Middle Term: Common term in premises (e.g., B).

4. Steps to Solve Syllogism Questions

  1. Identify the premises and conclusion (if provided).
  2. Draw Venn diagrams to visualize relationships between categories.
  3. Analyze the middle term to connect premises.
  4. Check if the conclusion follows logically from the premises.
  5. For possibility questions, consider all valid Venn diagrams.
  6. Verify by testing alternative diagrams or rules.

Example 1 (Standard Syllogism):

  • Premise 1: All cats are animals.
  • Premise 2: All animals are living beings.
  • Conclusion: All cats are living beings.
Is the conclusion valid?

Solution:

  • Draw Venn: Cats (C) inside Animals (A), A inside Living Beings (L).
  • Middle term: Animals.
  • Conclusion: C inside L → All C are L.
  • Answer: Valid.

[Cats inside Animals inside Living Beings]

Example 2 (Negative Statement):

  • Premise 1: No birds are mammals.
  • Premise 2: Some mammals are dogs.
  • Conclusion: No birds are dogs.
Is the conclusion valid?

Solution:

  • Venn: Birds (B) and Mammals (M) separate; some M overlap with Dogs (D).
  • Middle term: Mammals.
  • Since no B are M, and some M are D, no B are D (no overlap).
  • Answer: Valid.

[B separate from M; M overlaps D]

Example 3 (Possibility):

  • Premise 1: Some A are B.
  • Premise 2: Some B are C.
  • Conclusion: Some A are C.
Is the conclusion valid?

Solution:

  • Venn: A overlaps B; B overlaps C.
  • Middle term: B.
  • Possible diagrams: A, B, C may overlap, but A and C may not overlap.
  • Conclusion doesn’t always hold (e.g., A and C separate).
  • Answer: Not valid (but possible in some cases).

[A overlaps B; B overlaps C; A, C may or may not overlap]

5. Practice Questions

Question 1:

  • Premise 1: All roses are flowers.
  • Premise 2: Some flowers are red.
  • Conclusion: Some roses are red.
A) Valid B) Invalid C) Possible D) None

Solution:

  • Venn: Roses (R) inside Flowers (F); some F overlap Red (D).
  • Middle term: Flowers.
  • R may or may not overlap D.
  • Answer: B) Invalid (but possible).

Question 2:

  • Premise 1: No A are B.
  • Premise 2: All B are C.
  • Conclusion: No A are C.
A) Valid B) Invalid C) Possible D) None

Solution:

  • Venn: A separate from B; B inside C.
  • Middle term: B.
  • No A are B, and B are C → no A are C.
  • Answer: A) Valid.

Question 3:

  • Premise 1: Some P are Q.
  • Premise 2: No Q are R.
  • Conclusion: No P are R.
A) Valid B) Invalid C) Possible D) None

Solution:

  • Venn: P overlaps Q; Q separate from R.
  • Middle term: Q.
  • Some P are Q, no Q are R → P may overlap R.
  • Answer: B) Invalid.

6. Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Assuming "Some A are B" implies "Some A are not B" (not always true).
  • Misinterpreting "No A are B" as allowing overlap.
  • Not considering all possible Venn diagrams for possibility questions.
  • Ignoring the middle term’s role in connecting premises.
  • Confusing definite conclusions (e.g., "All") with possible ones.

7. Tips for Preparation

  • Practice drawing Venn diagrams for every question.
  • Memorize the four statement types (All, No, Some, Some not).
  • Solve 20–30 syllogism questions daily to improve speed.
  • Use the AEIO rule (Affirmative/Negative, Universal/Particular) to classify statements.
  • Check for complementary pairs in multiple-choice questions.

9. Advanced Syllogism Questions

Advanced questions involve three or more premises or coded statements.

Example (Three Premises):

  • Premise 1: All A are B.
  • Premise 2: Some B are C.
  • Premise 3: No C are D.
  • Conclusion: No A are D.

Solution:

  • Venn: A inside B; B overlaps C; C separate from D.
  • No C are D → no overlap with D.
  • A inside B, B may overlap C, but no C-D overlap → no A-D overlap.
  • Answer: Valid.

10. Practice Set (10 Questions)

Additional questions to reinforce learning:

Question 1:

  • Premise 1: All apples are fruits.
  • Premise 2: All fruits are healthy.
  • Conclusion: All apples are healthy.
A) Valid    B) Invalid    C) Possible    D) Can't say

Solution: Apples ⊂ Fruits ⊂ Healthy → Apples ⊂ Healthy → Answer: A) Valid

Question 2:

  • Premise 1: Some birds are parrots.
  • Premise 2: Some parrots are talkative.
  • Conclusion: Some birds are talkative.
A) Valid    B) Invalid    C) Possible    D) None

Solution: No direct link between birds and talkative. Answer: B) Invalid

Question 3:

  • Premise 1: All poets are dreamers.
  • Premise 2: No dreamers are realists.
  • Conclusion: No poets are realists.
A) Valid    B) Invalid    C) Can't say    D) Possible

Solution: Poets ⊂ Dreamers, and Dreamers ∩ Realists = ∅ → Poets ∩ Realists = ∅ → Answer: A) Valid

Question 4:

  • Premise 1: All X are Y.
  • Premise 2: No Y are Z.
  • Conclusion: No X are Z.
A) Valid    B) Invalid    C) Possible    D) None

Solution: X ⊂ Y, Y ∩ Z = ∅ → X ∩ Z = ∅ → Answer: A) Valid

Question 5:

  • Premise 1: Some cars are electric.
  • Premise 2: All electric are eco-friendly.
  • Conclusion: Some cars are eco-friendly.
A) Valid    B) Invalid    C) Possible    D) Can't say

Solution: Some cars ⊂ Electric ⊂ Eco-friendly → Conclusion is Possible but not definite → Answer: C) Possible

Question 6:

  • Premise 1: All actors are humans.
  • Premise 2: All humans are mammals.
  • Conclusion: All mammals are actors.
A) Valid    B) Invalid    C) Can't say    D) Possible

Solution: Reverse conclusion: Mammals ⊃ Humans ⊃ Actors, so can't say all mammals are actors. Answer: B) Invalid

Question 7:

  • Premise 1: No engineers are doctors.
  • Premise 2: All doctors are scientists.
  • Conclusion: No engineers are scientists.
A) Valid    B) Invalid    C) Possible    D) Can't say

Solution: No direct relation between engineers and scientists. Answer: D) Can't say

Question 8:

  • Premise 1: All books are papers.
  • Premise 2: Some papers are notes.
  • Conclusion: Some books are notes.
A) Valid    B) Invalid    C) Possible    D) Can't say

Solution: Books ⊂ Papers. Some Papers ⊂ Notes. No direct link between Books & Notes. Answer: D) Can't say

Question 9:

  • Premise 1: All students are intelligent.
  • Premise 2: Some students are hardworking.
  • Conclusion: Some intelligent people are hardworking.
A) Valid    B) Invalid    C) Possible    D) None

Solution: Some Students ⊂ Hardworking. Students ⊂ Intelligent → intersection exists. Conclusion is Possible. Answer: C) Possible

Question 10:

  • Premise 1: Some dogs are cats.
  • Premise 2: All cats are animals.
  • Conclusion: Some dogs are animals.
A) Valid    B) Invalid    C) Possible    D) Can't say

Solution: Some dogs ⊂ Cats ⊂ Animals → Some Dogs may be Animals → Possible but not definite. Answer: C) Possible
