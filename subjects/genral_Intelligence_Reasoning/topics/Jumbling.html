Jumbling - General Intelligence and Reasoning

1. Introduction to Jumbling

Jumbling, also known as rearrangement, is a reasoning topic that involves arranging jumbled words, sentences, or paragraphs in a logical order to form a meaningful sequence. It tests the ability to understand coherence, grammar, and logical flow. Jumbling questions are common in competitive exams to assess analytical and language skills.

Objective: Rearrange given elements (words, sentences, or paragraphs) to form a coherent and meaningful sequence.

Applications: Used in writing, editing, communication, and logical structuring of ideas.

2. Types of Jumbling Questions

Jumbling questions can be classified based on the elements to be rearranged:

  • Word Jumbling: Rearrange words to form a meaningful sentence (e.g., is / book / this / a → This is a book).
  • Sentence Jumbling: Arrange sentences to form a coherent paragraph.
  • Paragraph Jumbling: Rearrange paragraphs to form a logical passage.
  • Mixed Jumbling: Combine words and sentences or involve additional clues (e.g., first word is “The”).
  • Coded Jumbling: Sentences or words are coded, and rearrangement follows specific rules.

3. Key Concepts in Jumbling

Understanding the following concepts is essential for solving jumbling questions:

  • Grammar Rules: Subject-verb-object structure, articles, prepositions.
  • Logical Flow: Chronological order, cause-effect, or thematic progression.
  • Connectors: Words like “therefore,” “however,” or pronouns linking sentences.
  • Context Clues: Keywords indicating sequence (e.g., “firstly,” “finally”).

Articles (a, an, the) Often start sentences

4. Steps to Solve Jumbling Questions

  1. Identify the type of jumbling (word, sentence, or paragraph).
  2. Look for grammatical clues (e.g., articles, verbs) or logical connectors.
  3. Determine the starting element (e.g., sentence with “The” or introductory idea).
  4. Arrange elements to ensure coherence and logical flow.
  5. Test the sequence by reading it to check for meaning.
  6. Verify against options (if multiple-choice) or recheck for errors.

Example 1 (Word Jumbling): Arrange the words to form a meaningful sentence: is / book / this / a.

Solution:

  • Identify parts: “this” (pronoun), “is” (verb), “a” (article), “book” (noun).
  • Standard sentence: Subject + Verb + Object.
  • “a” and “book” form “a book”; “this” is subject; “is” is verb.
  • Sequence: This is a book.
  • Answer: This is a book.

Example 2 (Sentence Jumbling): Arrange the sentences:

  • A. She went to the market.
  • B. She bought some fruits.
  • C. She returned home.
  • D. She was hungry.

Solution:

  • Logical flow: Cause → Action → Result.
  • D (hungry) is cause; A (market) and B (bought fruits) are actions; C (returned) is result.
  • Sequence: D, A, B, C (She was hungry. She went to the market. She bought some fruits. She returned home).
  • Answer: DABC.

Example 3 (Coded Jumbling): Arrange the sentences where first sentence starts with “The”:

  • P. It was a sunny day.
  • Q. The team played well.
  • R. They won the match.
  • S. Fans cheered loudly.

Solution:

  • Q starts with “The” → Q is first.
  • P (sunny day) sets context; R (won match) follows Q; S (cheered) is result.
  • Sequence: Q, P, R, S.
  • Answer: QPRS.

5. Practice Questions

Question 1 (Word Jumbling): Arrange: was / reading / she / a / book.

A) She was reading a book. B) A book was she reading. C) She was a book reading. D) Reading was she a book.

Solution:

  • Parts: “she” (subject), “was” (auxiliary verb), “reading” (main verb), “a book” (object).
  • Sequence: She was reading a book.
  • Answer: A.

Question 2 (Sentence Jumbling): Arrange:

  • A. He finished his homework.
  • B. He was tired.
  • C. He went to bed.
  • D. He studied for hours.

A) ADBC B) BDAC C) DBAC D) ADCB

Solution:

  • Logical flow: D (studied) → A (finished) → B (tired) → C (bed).
  • Answer: C) DBAC.

Question 3 (Coded Jumbling): Arrange, given first sentence introduces topic:

  • P. It reduces pollution.
  • Q. Electric cars are popular.
  • R. They are eco-friendly.
  • S. Many people buy them.

A) QRSP B) QSPR C) PQRS D) RQPS

Solution:

  • Q introduces topic (electric cars); R (eco-friendly) explains; P (reduces pollution) elaborates; S (buy them) concludes.
  • Answer: A) QRSP.

6. Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Ignoring grammatical structure (e.g., placing verb before subject).
  • Missing logical connectors (e.g., “therefore,” “then”).
  • Not identifying the starting sentence or word.
  • Creating incoherent sequences by neglecting context.
  • Failing to recheck the final sequence for meaning.

7. Tips for Preparation

  • Practice basic grammar (subject-verb-object, articles).
  • Look for logical flow (cause-effect, chronological order).
  • Solve 20–30 jumbling questions daily to improve speed.
  • Use connectors and pronouns to link sentences.
  • Test sequences by reading aloud for coherence.

9. Advanced Jumbling Questions

Advanced questions involve longer paragraphs or complex clues.

Example (Paragraph Jumbling): Arrange:

  • A. It attracted many tourists.
  • B. The festival was vibrant.
  • C. Stalls sold local crafts.
  • D. Music filled the air.
  • E. It was held annually.

Solution:

  • E (annually) introduces; B (vibrant) describes; D (music) and C (stalls) elaborate; A (tourists) concludes.
  • Answer: EBDCA.
