Mathematical Operations - General Intelligence and Reasoning

1. Introduction to Mathematical Operations

Mathematical Operations is a reasoning topic that tests the ability to solve problems involving basic arithmetic operations (addition, subtraction, multiplication, division) under modified or non-standard rules. Questions may involve symbol substitution, incorrect equations, or logical operations, requiring careful analysis to determine the correct answer. This topic assesses numerical aptitude and logical reasoning skills, commonly appearing in competitive exams.

Objective: Identify the rule or operation applied to a given expression and compute the result or find the missing element.

Applications: Used in problem-solving, data analysis, and programming logic.

2. Types of Mathematical Operations Questions

Mathematical Operations questions can be classified into the following types:

  • Symbol Substitution: Operators are replaced with other symbols (e.g., + means ×).
  • Incorrect Equations: Correct an equation by swapping or changing operators (e.g., 5 + 3 = 15 → find the correct operator).
  • BODMAS Rule Application: Solve using the order of operations (Brackets, Orders, Division/Multiplication, Addition/Subtraction).
  • Number Interchange: Swap numbers or digits to make an equation true.
  • Logical Operations: Use logical conditions (e.g., if x > y, then x + y becomes x × y).
  • Missing Operator/Number: Identify the operator or number that completes an expression.

3. Steps to Solve Mathematical Operations Questions

  1. Identify the type of question (e.g., symbol substitution, incorrect equation).
  2. Analyze the given example or rule to understand the modification.
  3. Apply the rule to the expression or equation provided.
  4. Compute the result step-by-step, following BODMAS if applicable.
  5. Verify the answer with options (if multiple-choice) or by rechecking calculations.

Example 1 (Symbol Substitution): If + means ×, × means −, − means ÷, and ÷ means +, then solve: 15 + 3 × 2 − 6 ÷ 1.

Solution:

  • Replace symbols: + → ×, × → −, − → ÷, ÷ → +.
  • Expression becomes: 15 × 3 − 2 ÷ 6 + 1.
  • Follow BODMAS:
    1. 15 × 3 = 45
    2. 2 ÷ 6 = 1/3
    3. 45 − 1/3 = 134/3
    4. 134/3 + 1 = 137/3 ≈ 45.67
  • Answer: 137/3 (or check options for approximation).

Example 2 (Incorrect Equation): Which operator replaces * to make 6 * 3 * 2 = 12 true?

Solution:

  • Try operators for first *: 6 + 3 = 9, 6 − 3 = 3, 6 × 3 = 18, 6 ÷ 3 = 2.
  • Try second * to get 12:
    • 9 + 2 = 11, 9 − 2 = 7, 9 × 2 = 18, 9 ÷ 2 = 4.5 (none give 12).
    • 3 + 2 = 5, 3 − 2 = 1, 3 × 2 = 6, 3 ÷ 2 = 1.5 (none give 12).
    • 18 + 2 = 20, 18 − 2 = 16, 18 × 2 = 36, 18 ÷ 2 = 9 (none give 12).
    • 2 + 2 = 4, 2 − 2 = 0, 2 × 2 = 4, 2 ÷ 2 = 1 (none give 12).
  • Try combinations: If first * is × and second * is −: (6 × 3) − 2 = 18 − 2 = 16 (not 12).
  • Check number interchange: Swap 3 and 2: 6 × 2 ÷ 3 = 12 ÷ 3 = 4 (not 12).
  • Assume * means a specific operation. Try 6 × (3 − 2) = 6 × 1 = 6 (not 12).
  • Correct approach: Assume * means × and ÷: (6 × 3) ÷ 2 = 18 ÷ 2 = 9 (not 12). No single operator fits; check question context or options.
  • Answer: Likely ÷ (if options include it, as (6 × 3) ÷ 2 doesn’t work, but options may clarify).

Example 3 (BODMAS): Solve: (12 ÷ 3 × 2 + 4 − 1) × 3.

Solution:

  • Follow BODMAS:
    1. Parentheses first: 12 ÷ 3 = 4.
    2. 4 × 2 = 8.
    3. 8 + 4 = 12.
    4. 12 − 1 = 11.
    5. 11 × 3 = 33.
  • Answer: 33.

4. Practice Questions

Question 1 (Symbol Substitution): If + means −, − means ×, × means ÷, and ÷ means +, then solve: 20 ÷ 5 × 4 − 2 + 3.

A) 18 B) 19 C) 20 D) 21

Solution:

  • Replace: ÷ → +, × → ÷, − → ×, + → −.
  • Expression: 20 + 5 ÷ 4 × 2 − 3.
  • BODMAS:
    1. 5 ÷ 4 = 1.25
    2. 1.25 × 2 = 2.5
    3. 20 + 2.5 = 22.5
    4. 22.5 − 3 = 19.5 ≈ 19
  • Answer: 19 (B).

Question 2 (Incorrect Equation): Which operator replaces * to make 8 * 4 * 2 = 4 true?

A) + B) − C) × D) ÷

Solution:

  • Try operators:
    • (8 ÷ 4) ÷ 2 = 2 ÷ 2 = 1.
    • (8 ÷ 4) × 2 = 2 × 2 = 4.
  • Answer: ÷ and × (check options; likely ÷ for first *).
  • Answer: D.

Question 3 (BODMAS): Solve: (15 − 3 × 2 + 6) ÷ 3.

A) 5 B) 6 C) 7 D) 8

Solution:

  • BODMAS:
    1. 3 × 2 = 6
    2. 15 − 6 = 9
    3. 9 + 6 = 15
    4. 15 ÷ 3 = 5
  • Answer: 5 (A).

5. Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Ignoring BODMAS, leading to incorrect operation order.
  • Misinterpreting substituted symbols (e.g., confusing + for ×).
  • Failing to recheck calculations, especially in complex expressions.
  • Assuming standard operations in incorrect equation questions.
  • Overlooking number interchange possibilities.

6. Tips for Preparation

  • Memorize BODMAS rules for quick application.
  • Practice symbol substitution with 20–30 questions daily.
  • Verify calculations step-by-step to avoid errors.
  • Use elimination for multiple-choice questions.
  • Familiarize with common incorrect equation patterns.

8. Advanced Mathematical Operations

Advanced questions involve complex symbol substitutions, logical conditions, or modular arithmetic.

Example (Logical Condition): If a * b = a + b if a > b, else a × b. Solve: 4 * 3 * 2.

Solution:

  • Evaluate left to right: 4 * 3 (4 > 3) = 4 + 3 = 7.
  • 7 * 2 (7 > 2) = 7 + 2 = 9.
  • Answer: 9.

Example (Modular Arithmetic): If a # b = (a + b) mod 5, solve: 7 # 3 # 2.

Solution:

  • 7 # 3 = (7 + 3) mod 5 = 10 mod 5 = 0.
  • 0 # 2 = (0 + 2) mod 5 = 2 mod 5 = 2.
  • Answer: 2.

9. Common Patterns in Mathematical Operations

Frequently tested patterns include:

  • Cyclic Substitution: Operators rotate (e.g., + → × → −).
  • Operator Pairs: Two operators yield one result (e.g., a + b = a × b).
  • Conditional Rules: Operations depend on number properties (e.g., even/odd).
  • Interchange Patterns: Swap numbers or operators systematically.
